# Fabio Akita

Fábio Akita is a prominent figure in the Brazilian software engineering community, creating dense, highly technical content centered around computer science fundamentals, historical timelines in computing, and modern infrastructure.

## Content Philosophy: "Aprenda a Aprender" (Learn to Learn)
Akita's central theme is the rejection of superficial knowledge and the pursuit of first principles. 
- **Desmistificação (Debunking)**: He actively works to filter "signal from noise" in tech hype, particularly regarding AI and Quantum Computing. His stance is that most tech news (e.g., "Scientist says...") is often 90% noise or corporate marketing.
- **Foundational Rigor**: He advocates for understanding the "why" behind technologies—how they work under the hood, their historical origin, and the physical constraints (hardware, math) that govern them.
His approach demystifies high-level abstractions by grounding them in first principles, such as explaining modern gaming emulator constraints through the lens of 6502 processors, memory maps, and hardware limitations. 

He maintains an extensive personal infrastructure, including NAS arrays and homelabs, often pushing the boundaries of Linux networking, containers, and GPU utilization for both AI testing and gaming. Recent workflows involve using models like [[Artificial_Intelligence/Claude_Code.md|Claude Code]] to define infrastructure as code.

### The 2026 "Point of Turning"
In early 2026, Akita underwent a period of intense **hyperfocus** on Large Language Models (LLMs), spending over 500 hours experimenting with 30+ different models.
- **Workflow Evolution**: During this period, he transitioned to an "Agentic-first" developmental flow, leveraging specialized harnesses (Rédias) to eliminate friction in areas he traditionally builds cautiously (e.g., HTML/CSS). He adopted the [[Programming/Vibe_Coding.md|Vibe_Coding]] terminology, but redefined it as a high-precision engineering process.
- **Technical Pedantry**: Akita argues that the secret to using LLMs effectively is extreme technical precision. Instead of vague feedback like "the font is too small," he provides descriptors like **kerning**, **line-height**, or **vertical/horizontal orientation**, forcing the model to operate within technical constraints rather than "vibes."
- **LLM Coding Benchmark**: Dissatisfied with standard industry benchmarks, he developed a personal benchmarking suite focused on full-project integrity rather than isolated code snippets.
- **AGI Skepticism**: He remains a vocal critic of the "Artificial General Intelligence" narrative, calling it a marketing label. He points to the **mathematical impossibility of absolute precision** in binary computation (e.g., the inability of floating point values like $0.1 + 0.2$ to equal $0.3$) as a fundamental proof that computers are limited by the physical and logical constraints of their design, not by "intelligence."
- **Open Source Advocacy**: Documented this entire 300,000+ lines of code journey in his blog (omness.com) and opened 16+ new projects on GitHub, reinforcing his "show the code" philosophy.

## Formative Years (1990-1995)

Akita's entry into technology defines his philosophy of "learning by doing" and curiosity-driven mastery.

### 1. Earliest Experience (1990-1994)
- **Hardward Constraints**: Started on a PC XT (8MHz, 1MB RAM, 10MB Winchester HD) running MS-DOS.
- **Language Stack**: Learned GW-Basic by copying code from books; moved to **Clipper** and **dBase III** for building professional inventory systems for his uncle.
- **Self-Taught Illustration**: Mastered vector illustration in CorelDraw in a single month to get published in a magazine, illustrating his ability to learn under pressure.

### 2. University and Hacker Culture (1995)
- **Entry in USP**: 1995. Exposure to the university's network infrastructure, which involved physical cabling (coaxial), HP-UX terminals, and the early Brazilian internet.
- **Gateway to Mastery**: Driven by a "ignoble" goal—speeding up **warez** (pirated software) downloads—he learned phreaking (Blue Boxes), password cracking (passwd/shadow files), and Unix administration to gain higher-speed shell access.
- **Tools of the Era**: BBS, IRC, Usenet, Lynx, and Mosaic. This exposure to low-level networking and security foundations remains a core part of his content strategy.
